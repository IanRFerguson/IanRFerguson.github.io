About Me

I am a social scientist at Stanford University, where I work as the Lab Manager for the Social Neuroscience Lab under the direction of Dr. Jamil Zaki. Principally, I am interested in taking data-driven approaches to exploring the relationship between hierarchical status threat and use of hate speech on social media. Other projects I am actively involved in include modeling neural responses to simulated social support as a buffering mechanism to negative affect, as well as quantifying moral language usage in the US congress as a predictor of party voting behavior.

In addition to my academic work, I am a developer and data consultant at Red Bridge Strategies, a progressive consulting firm based in San Francisco. My work in this capacity includes everything from building interactive mapping applications to compiling voter information and scripting text and email blasts. I'm passionate about using data and technology to make the world a more equitable place!

For a primer on this work, see my Master's Thesis , developed at NYU under the guidance of Dr. Jon Freeman and Dr. DongWon Oh.

Selected Projects

Ambient Demographic Shifts as Predictors of Hate Speech

In an ongoing project, I am attempting to quantify the degree to which local demographic changes lead to higher rates of hate speech on social media sites (namely Twitter).

GLM Express

GLM Express is a lightweight, object-oriented approach to modeling functional neuroimaging data. It includes Subject and GroupLevel objects, and is optimized to work on any BIDS validated project.

NYU Master's Thesis

Taking an experimental approach, I measured the cognitive effects of status threat in non-Hispanic white individuals. After a threat prime, participants rated ensembles of mixed-race groups on several dimensions. A GEE model offered evidence for a linear trend, such that as the number of Hispanic social targets increased the group was rated as more threatening.

Quantifying Debate Performance

Using a custom Python script, I scraped tweets mentioning Trump or Biden every minute throughout the first General Election debate in 2020. Tweets were tokenized, and word stems were assessed for AFINN polarity values.
